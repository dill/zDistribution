Running page head: Depth distribution of rare target detections

Introduction

All analyses of community composition face a similar challenge: rare species dropout. This leads to inaccurate estimates of biodiversity or community composition and greater difficulty assessing changes in ecosystem resilience, as well as inaccurate characterization of a rare species distribution and greater difficulty assessing changes in distribution in response to environmental perturbations.

Many methodological approaches have been developed to address this problem, both in the experimental design phase (REFS) and in the data analysis phase (REFS). Statistical approaches aim to quantify and minimize the effect of rare species dropout, while sampling approaches aim to reduce the rate of dropout via robust sampling design. Within the latter category, it has been shown that adopting an ensemble approach, i.e. integrating across multiple types of observation methods in order to smooth across the weaknesses of each approach individually, often produces more robust estimates of species’ distributions and community composition than using single methods alone (REFS).

eDNA is emerging as a viable tool for improving spatial distribution models for rare marine species, especially when used jointly with existing sampling methods, e.g. net tows, active acoustics, and/or bottom trawls for fishes and invertebrates. To date, these methods have been most frequently used to assess biodiversity and species’ xy distributions in lakes, streams. Their use is growing in broad scale marine studies (e.g. Shelton 2019? others?), studies of 3D distributions (REF?), and tracking rare species (REF?). In the case of marine mammals - notably some of the rarest species in the marine environment - two survey techniques are traditionally used. Visual line-transect surveys are the most common, in which observers aboard a ship or airplane will record detections of any marine mammals and their distance to the transect line, covering the survey area in a systematic pattern. These surveys are limited to the surface of the ocean, where most marine mammals spend very little of their time, and to daylight hours - and their effectiveness decreases in poor weather or sea state. Ship-board passive acoustic line-transect surveys collect recordings along similar systematic transects, and can often detect species up to XX kilometers away from the ship in any direction. They have some of the same limitations at visual surveys, but only detect species that are actively vocalizing. Systematic eDNA surveys, where water samples are collected along transects, do not share limitations related to depth, daylight, weather conditions, or species’ vocal activity, but notably have a detection range that is much smaller than either of the two previous surveys and strongly affected by diffusion and transport by oceanographic features (REFS).

In open ocean, deep water environments, our understanding of species’ distributions may be affected by sampling depth, a fundamental aspect of experimental design often overlooked in 3D environments. This is especially true for mobile species capable of utilizing a broad swath of the water column. In the pelagic marine environment, some rare species have depth distributions spanning 3km or greater (CITE Greg paper), which complicates decision-making regarding where to collect point-samples with the aim of describing x-y distribution, either independently of or alongside z-distribution.

Because eDNA samples have a limited radius of detection, and because they are costly both to collect and to process, studies targeting a specific set of rare species may benefit from restricting their range of sampling depths, especially when describing x-y distribution has a higher priority than describing z-distribution. However, there is currently a lack of published data providing guidance regarding the range of sampling depths that can be used to target certain species, taxonomic groups, or ecological groups of rare species, or what ecological or observational processes may drive variability in the probability of detecting specific species across depths.

  • [3D novelty point] Further, eDNA promises new capability to monitor spatiotemporal variability in vertical habitat use that, when combined with variability in x-y habitat use, may improve novel insight into habitat use for species with 3D distributions. If the vertical distribution of eDNA faithfully reflects the vertical distribution of rare species, this tool may become a viable non-invasive option for understanding 3D habitat use of rare and elusive species on a broad scale.

Here, we aim improve our understanding of the depth distribution of rare species’ eDNA in the water column and identify major environmental and ecological drivers of observed patterns. We specifically ask three questions:

  1. Does cetacean eDNA distribution vary in the z-dimension? If so, what major ecological factors (e.g. preferred prey, time spent at depth, or phylogeny) can be used to predict cetacean eDNA distribution across depths?
  2. Do common sources of observational bias, e.g. technical replication, choice of primers, or sampling volume, interact with sampling depth to affect probability of detection?
  3. Does x-y variability in eDNA distribution interact with depth variability to affect probability of detection?

To answer these questions, we utilize eDNA (water) samples collected in the California Current in 2019 in order to assess the abundance of North Pacific hake* (Merluccius productus). We leverage this opportunistic dataset to examine marine mammal detections generated via genetic metabarcoding of three mitochondrial target regions. By collapsing eDNA detections from these three primers across x and y dimensions and focusing only on the z-distribution of sampling effort (0-500 m), we aim to determine (1) whether species- or taxon-specific detection probability varies with depth and (2) how depth-dependent detection probability varies with primer specificity, technical replication, or DNA degradation caused by freeze/thaw cycles. Finally, we explicitly re-introduce x-y dimensions in order to determine (3) whether depth-dependent detection probability varies with latitude or longitude.

Methods

Sample Collection

We leveraged eDNA samples collected on the 2019 US-Canada Integrated Ecosystems and Acoustics-Trawl Survey for Pacific hake from 2 July to 19 August 2019. The study area is encompassed by the California Current System, which is 500-800km wide, extends from 43°N to 23°N, and comprises an equatorward surface current and a subsurface poleward current that is concentrated mainly on the continental shelf (Hickey 1979). The system is characterized by strong alongshore advection, inshore upwelling concentrated primarily along the continental shelf break, and multiple terrestrial and oceanic water sources Auad, Roemmich, and Gilson (2011). These complex oceanogrpahic processes support a highly productive and diverse assemblage of plankton and fishes, form a rich prey base for at least 25 cetacean species that inhabit the study area (Carretta et al. 2023).

A full description of field sampling and DNA isolation are provided in Shelton et al. (2022). Briefly summarized, seawater was collected at 186 stations from six depths (surface, 50, 100, 150, 300 and 500 m) using niskin bottles attached to a CTD rosette (totalling 1769 samples). All eDNA sampling occurred at night. 2.5 L of water was filtered across a 47-mm diameter, 1-µm pore size mixed cellulose ester (MCE) filter, using a vacuum filtration manifold connected to a vacuum pump. The filters were immediately preserved in Longmire’s buffer and stored at room temperature until extraction.

In total, we selected 572 samples from the dataset described above. The samples selected were collected from 182 stations, spanning south-north from latitudes 37.89499°N to 48.39881°N and west-east 126.4298°W to 122.8638°W. In terms of depth, we selected 186 were from the surface (0 m), 163 from 50 m, 74 from 150 m, 75 from 300 m, and 74 from 500 m. Samples were randomized across working plates, and were stored in the freezer when not in use. We selected the dilution for the sample that was determined from the qPCR data (described above) to be not inhibited, which was either 1x or 0.2x.

Sample Processing

DNA was extracted using a phase-lock phenol chloroform method as described in Ramón-Laca et al. (2021). All samples were tested for inhibition by spiking an internal positive control (IPC) of known concentration into each sample and quantifying it using a probe assay with quantitative PCR (qPCR). Samples were deemed inhibited if the IPC concentration was lower than expected (e.g., a Ct cycle value of 0.5 or higher than the expected Ct) - as described in Shelton et al. (2022).

We amplified each sample with three different primer sets: MiFish-U (Miya et al. 2015), MarVer1 (Valsecchi et al. 2020) and D-loop (baker_etal06?). For all samples, we ran one technical PCR replicate for MiFish-U and three technical replicates for MarVer1. We ran one technical PCR replicate for all samples with D-loop, except the 0 and 50 m samples along the WA coast, for which we ran three technical replicates. Primer, Taq, and cycling conditions were chosen after extensive testing on mock communities, see Shaffer et al. (2025).

For each primer set, we amplified DNA in the first PCR reaction (PCR1) in a 20 μL reaction consisting of: 10 μL of Phusion Master Mix (2X), 0.4 μL of the forward primer (10 μM); 0.4 μL of the reverse primer (10 μM), 0.6 μL of 100% DMSO, 0.5 μL of rAlbumin (20 μg/μL), 4.4 μL of nuclease-free water and 2 μL of DNA template. Reactions were run with the following cycling conditions: an initial denaturation of 98°C for 30 sec; followed by cycles of: denaturation (98°C for 10 sec), annealing (60°C for 30 sec), and extension (72°C for 3 sec); with a final extension of 72°C for 10 min and hold at 4°C. We ran 35 cycles for MiFish-U and MarVer1, and 40 cycles for D-loop.

For MiFish-U and MarVer1, despite running the dilution identified by qPCR as uninhibited, some samples failed to produce a visible band on a 2% agarose gel after PCR2 (see next section). When this occurred, we diluted the sample and reran it until a visible band was produced. For D-loop, because the target was not expected in every sample unlike fish and vertebrates, we could not distinguish between an inhibited sample (i.e., one that did not produce a band) or a negative sample (e.g., one that was absent of cetaceans). Here we assumed that the dilution given by qPCR was not inhibited and treated these as negative detections.

For Mifish-U and MarVer1, samples that returned less than 100 total reads were rerun in an effort to complete the dataset to include one MiFish-U replicate and three MarVer1 replicates. For some samples, we tried up to six replicates.

Library Preparation and Sequencing

PCR product was cleaned using Ampure Beads (1.2x beads:product ratio, with two 70% ethanol washes) and then indexed in a second PCR reaction (PCR2), with the following recipe: 12.5 μL of KAPA HiFi HotStart ReadyMix (Roche Diagnostics), 1.25 μL of an index from IDT for Illumina DNA/RNA UD Indexes (Sets A-D), PCR1 product and nuclease free water. For MiFish-U and MarVer1, we added 5 μL PCR1 product, and 6.25 μL of nuclease free water, and for D-loop, we added 11.25 μL PCR1 product and no nuclease free water. Cycling conditions included an initial denaturation of 95°C for 5 min; cycles of: 98°C for 20 sec, 56°C for 30 sec, 72°C for 1 min; and a final extension of 72°C for 5 min. We ran 35 cycles for MiFish-U and MarVer1, and 12 cycles for D-loop.

Resulting products were visualized on a 2% agarose gel and quantified using Quant-iT™ dsDNA Assay Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA) with Fluoroskan™ Microplate Fluorometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA). Indexed products were normalized by concentration, pooled into libraries for sequencing, and then size- selected to extract only the target band using the E-Gel™ SizeSelect™ II Agarose Gels (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA). Subsequently the libraries were sequenced on Illumina MiSeq platform at the University of Washington using the v3 600 cycle kit. We loaded libraries 6-8 pM with a 20% PhiX spike in. In total, we ran 36 sequencing runs from May 2023 to July 2025.

For every sequencing run, we included negative and positive controls. We either included nuclease-free water (negative control) or genomic DNA from kangaroo (positive control) to PCR1 and carried it through the rest of the library preparation workflow. If anything other than human or NA was found in the negative control, or if kangaroo appeared in any other eDNA sample, we omitted that plate from the final analysis - which occurred for only one plate.

Bioinformatic Processing

We analyzed all sequencing data using the bioinformatics workflow established in Guri et al. (2025). In short, Illumina-demultiplexed reads were trimmed with Cutadapt v4.9 to remove primer and adapter sequences, allowing a maximum mismatch rate of 0.1 (Martin, 2011). Quality filtering and minimum-length truncation were performed with the DADA2 filterAndTrim function, and amplicon sequence variants (ASVs) were inferred using default denoising settings (Callahan et al. 2016).

Following initial QAQC of the raw sequence data, each unique ASV was queried against a vertebrate mitochondrial subset of the January 2025 NCBI nr eukaryote database using standalone BLAST configured with “-word_size 20 -evalue 1e-30 -max_target_seqs 100” (Altschul et al., 1990), supplemented with a negative-taxid file to remove taxa with distributions outside the North Pacific Ocean. Taxonomic assignments were determined using a least-common-ancestor procedure in TaxonKit v0.17 (Shen & Ren, 2021), prioritizing exact-identity matches and otherwise applying a stepwise identity threshold (>99.3%, >98%). Marine mammal identifications were then manually reviewed to ensure consistency with regional species distributions. ASVs suspected to represent nuclear mitochondrial pseudogenes were omitted from downstream analyses.

Detection Data QAQC

We defined a sample as a station/depth/technical replicate combination. A subset of samples were sequenced at multiple dilutions (i.e., 1:1, 1:5, or 1:10) or multiple times in order to maximize sequencing success; for these samples, we retained the iteration with the greatest number of total reads.

To obtain positive detections for each sample, we kept any species with > 1 sequencing read. We filtered out any non-target (non-cetacean) species, and also removed detections of bottlenose dolphins, considered likely contamination, and unidentified Balaenopterids or Delphinids.

To obtain non-detections, we included all MFU and MV1 samples that had at least one marine species detected, but no cetaceans - excluding any samples that completely failed sequencing. Because our DL primer specifically targets a subset of very rare species, we assume that sequencing failure indicates a non-detection - therefore, for this primer we included any station/depth/species combination that was not detected as a non-detect.

Data Analysis

Question 1: Does POD vary with depth?

We addressed this question using generalized additive models [GAMs; Wood (2017)] implemented using the mgcv package in R (R Core Team 2016). GAMs have previously been demonstrated to be an appropriate tool for modeling spatial use in cetaceans (e.g. Elizabeth A. Becker et al. (2016); Elizabeth A. Becker et al. (2019); Elizabeth A. Becker et al. (2020); Elizabeth A. Becker et al. (2022); Abrahms et al. (2019)). We fit all GAMs using 0/1 detections as the response variable with a binomial distribution and logit link function, and estimated model parameters using REML. Models examining depth effect alone were built iteratively, starting with (1) assessing probability of detection (POD) across all species at sampling depths between 1 and 500m, then (2) allowing y-intercept to vary by species, and finally (3) allowing model smooth to vary by species.

In addition to examining depth variability across species-level strata, we examined several other ecological stratification schemes in order to determine whether depth variability in detection probability is driven by (1) evolutionary inertia (i.e. taxonomic family) or (2) preferred prey type.

We assessed model fit using AIC, and model performance using standard metrics including the percent of deviance explained, the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC, Fawcett 2006), the true skill statistic (TSS, Allouche, Tsoar, and Kadmon 2006), and a visual comparison of predicted vs. observed detection rates across sampling depths. We also visually compared our model-based predictions of probability of detection across depth with estimates of species-specific time-at-depth as reported in (Watwood and Buonantony 2012) and (Navy 2017).

Question 2: Does accounting for detectability change inference on depth relationship?

In Q1, we did not account for the primers used when processing each sample. Since primers were applied consistently across samples, as described in section X.X, this is not likely to affect our results. However, we were interested in leveraging the information contained in multiple technical replicates from individual biological samples to better understand the occupancy, capture, and detection processes for cetacean eDNA. We used a Bayesian hierarchical occupancy model to separately estimate the probability of occupancy/capture by depth and detectability by primer.

GAMs can also be used in a Bayesian context (Miller 2025). To incorporate smooth terms in our Bayesian hierarchical model we exported the design, penalty, and covariance matrices created in model Q1.3 using mgcv::jagam (Wood, N Pya, and Safken 2016).

Our occupancy model was modified from Patin et al. (submitted) and consisted of two levels: occupancy/capture (eDNA presence) and detection.

We modelled the presence of cetacean eDNA in a given biological replicate at site \(s\) and depth \(d\), \(z_{s,d}\), as a Bernoulli random variable with occurrence probability \(\Psi_d\):

\[ z_{s,d} \sim \text{Bernoulli}(\Psi_{d}) \],

where probability of presence in a sample at depth \(\Psi_d\) is a smooth, species-specific function of sample depth \(D_d\):

\[ \text{logit}(\Psi_d) = s(d) \cdot D_d. \]

Each biological replicate was then subsampled for PCR amplification with three different primer sets. We modelled the observed detection of cetacean eDNA in each technical replicate \(x_t\) as a Bernoulli random variable with primer-specific detection probability \(\Omega_\text{primer}\) conditional on presence in the biological sample \(z_{s,d}\):

\[ x_t \sim \text{Bernoulli}({\Omega_\text{primer}} \cdot z_{s,d}). \]

Question 3: Does depth variability in POD covary with xy variability?

We returned to the use of GAMs in a frequentist context to address Question 3, and again built iterative models in order to examine covariability between depth, latitude, and longitude on a per-species basis. We began by examining whether xy variability explained a significant amount of remaining variability left in the model after depth variability was account for (i.e. POD ~ z + xy), then tested whether x, y, and z covary (i.e. POD ~ xyz). Finally, we allowed the model of xyz covariability to vary idependently by species.

We assessed model fit using AIC, and model performance using percent deviance explained, AUC, and TSS, as in Question 1. We then conducted a visual comparison of the predicted depth of maximum POD across and our observed detection depth within the study area for the most frequently observed baleen whale, dolphin, and beaked whale.

Results

We selected 516 water samples collected at 175 unique locations during the 2019 NOAA hake abundance survey (Figure 1), targeting 0, 50, 150, 300, and 500 m sampling depths at each station. Where the bottom was less than 500 m deep, we did not collect a sample at 500 m. We amplified and sequenced these samples in 1 technical replicate using MFU, 3 technical replicates using MV1, and 1 technical replicate using DL, resulting in a final total of 1548 amplified and sequenced samples.

Across all samples, we observed 468 positive detections of 16 cetacean species, including 6 baleen whale species, 5 dolphin species, 2 porpoise species, and 3 beaked whale species (Table 1). 11 species were observed more than 10 times, and and additional 5 were observed fewer than 10 times. This includes one detection of a bowhead whale (Balaena mysticetus), as well as three detections of saber-toothed whales (Mesoplodon stejnegeri). Figure 1 illustrates the distribution of detections across sampling depths, ignoring x-y distribution, for all cetacean species included in the study.

Question 1: Does POD vary with depth?

GAM-based model selection of detection probability across sampling depths negated the null hypothesis of no variation across depth (Supplemental Figure S1). The best alternative model includes significant smooth terms for depth, species, and depth x species interaction (AIC = 4547, Table 2). This model explained 14.3% of variance in eDNA detection probability, with a mean AUC of 0.73 and a mean optimized TSS of 0.38. Full model TSS for all thresholds, as well as per-species AUC, optimized TSS, TSS values across all thresholds are shown in Supplemental Table S2 and Supplemental Figure S2.

Broadly, delphinids were more likely to be detected at the surface, beaked whales at depths > 200m, and baleen whales either at the surface or at depths > 400m (Figure 2). While detection data from the five species detected fewer than 10 times were included in our model input, we did not make predictions for any of these species. Species-specific model predictions generally agreed with the raw detection data.

Among the delphinids, species-specific trends in POD generally reflected model estimates of time-at-depth. Conversely, most beaked whale and baleen whale species in the study were most likely to be detected deeper than where they spend most of their time (Figure 2). Most notably, predicted POD-at-depth plots for two of the three baleen whales indicated a high probability of detection at 500 m, while these two species spend most of their time above ~200 m. Per-species model predictions are shown alongside observed detections in Supplemental Figure S5.

Models stratifying detections by taxonomic Family and preferred prey type both had lower AIC values than the model stratified by species (AIC = 4854 and 4960, respectively); however, these models still had significant terms for Family and depth x Family interactions (taxonomic model) and for depth, prey type, and depth x prey type interactions (prey type model). Predicted POD curves for each model illustrate varying trends in POD with depth across strata (Figure 3). Similar to the model stratified by species, we did not make predictions for Families with fewer than 10 detections using the taxonomic model, which excluded the Balaenidae (bowhead whale family).

Question 2: Does depth variability in POD covary with observation bias?

Our hierarchical model indicates that given the presence of a cetacean species’ eDNA in a sample, the probability of detecting that DNA in downstream processing is affected by primer specificity, DNA degradation by freeze/thaw cycles, and the number of technical replicates used to used to probe the sample (?@fig-H2POD-obsbias). Sequencing runs using MV1 and MFU primers, which target regions of XX and XX bp in length, were not affected by the number of freeze-thaw cycles accumulated prior to sequencing. Sequencing runs using the DL primer set, which targets a region of XX bp in length, was affected by freeze thaw cycles such that sequencing runs with 1 freeze/thaw cycle had a higher detection rate than MV1 or MFU and sequencing runs with 2-XX freeze/thaw cycles had a lower detection rate thn MV1 or MFU.

Question 3: Does depth variability in POD covary with xy variability?

Adding xy covariates to the best fit model from Q1 improved overall model performance. Our model included terms for species, lat/lon, and depth, as well as species x lat/lon, species x depth, and species x depth x lat/lon (AIC = 4200.509007), and explained 25.3% of the variability in eDNA detection. Although terms for depth and lat/lon were not significant, removing these terms increased model AIC to 4947. Mean AUC across a 10-fold validation test was 0.78 and mean optimized TSS was 0.45. Full model TSS for all thresholds are shown in Supplemental Table S2 and Supplemental Figure S2.

Predicted vertical probability of detection for the most frequently detected delphinid, baleen whale, and beaked whale varied with latitute and longitude, while adhering to the broad trends identified in Q1 (Figure 6). The Pacific white-side dolphin is most likely to be detected near the surface, with max POD varying between depths of 0 m and 410 m. Generally, detections of Pacific white-sided dolphins that were farther south and east were also more likely to be deeper in the water column.

Depths of highest POD for humpback whales had two peaks around 0 m and 500 m; and depths of highest POD for Baird’s beaked whales varied between 460 m and 470 m. Lat/lon variability in Baird’s beaked whale detections followed a similar pattern to Pacific white-sided dolphins, with greater likelihood of deeper detections in stations that are more to the south and east. The two peaks in depth of greatest POD for humpback whales were roughly distributed with higher probability of detection around 0 m on the continental shelf, and higher probability of detection around 500 m on the shelf break.

In addition to examining geographic patterns in the depth of maximum POD, we examined uncertainty around these values by mapping the range of depths that fell within the 50% confidence interval of maximum POD (Figure 6, bottom). In the northern portion of the study area, where Pacific white-sided dolphins are most likely to be observed near the surface, the range of depths within the 50% CI of maximum POD is small (minimum depth range = 10 m). That range increases toward the south and east (maximum depth range = 500 m), as the depth of maximum POD gets deeper for this species. Baird’s beaked whales exhibited the opposite trend: the range of depths falling within the 50% CI of maximum POD was smallest in the southest portion of the range (minimum depth range = 10 m), where the depth of maximum POD was deepest, and increased in the northern part of the range (maximum depth range = 470 m). The bimodal peak of maximum POD observed in humpback whale eDNA was mirrored in the range of depths within the 50% CI of maximum POD: the depth range was largest in the transition zones between the two peaks (maximum depth range = 500 m), and relatively small outside of the transition zones (minimum depth range = 0 m).

Discussion

Question 1:

In addition to being extremely rare targets, marine mammals are highly mobile in xy space, and their vertical ranges span 100s to 1,000s of meters. Mapping the vertical distribution of their eDNA can provide new insight regarding their 3D spatial use, including seasonal or geographic variability in depth distribution. Additionally, eDNA monitoring has the potential to improve our understanding of xy spatial distribution, especially for visually or acoustically elusive species, when used in joint species distribution models. In these cases, optimizing the sampling depth to target the species of interest could increase detection rates and improve xy distributional models while minimizing cost.

POD varied significantly with sampling depth (Supplemental Figure S1), and was best described at a species-specific level, and broadly reflected our current understanding of species-specific variability in use of the water column (Figure 2). This was especially true among the delphinids and porpoises; on the other hand, we observed some noticeable deviations from expected trends in both the baleen whales and the beaked whales. These trends could be inherent to the behavior of the animals themselves, or more likely may reflect the “behavior” of eDNA.

For example, baleen whales are generally thought to be shallow divers, and while some dives up to approximately 500 m have been recorded in a few species (e.g. fin whales, Panigada et al. 1999), recent reports of time-at-depth for cetacean species indicates that baleen whales spend the vast majority of their time within the top 100-200 m of the ocean. However, we observe a sharp spike in detection probability at 500 m for two of the three baleen whale species included in our model predictions. Gray whales were not likely to be observed at 500 m; this is most likely because they are a nearshore coastal species that is rarely observed in deep water. Humpback and fin whales both have pelagic distributions, and were both as likely to be observed at 500 m as they were at the surface, with low POD at intermediate depths.

This observed pattern has several possible explanations. It is possible that some of these deeper eDNA detections, especially those of deeper-diving fin whales, are detections of deep dives made by animals foraging for krill at depth. However, based on our current knowledge of the diving limits of baleen whales, we consider it highly unlikely that most of the deep detections are indicative of the presence of the detected species at that depth. We considered the possibility that the large spike in deep detections was driven by whalefall or predation (i.e. whole or parts of animals sinking in the water column). In total, baleen whales were detected below 250 m at 28 locations; of those, 8 detections (28.5%) were within 100 m of the ocean floor and could be potential detections of whalefall. A map of potential whalefall locations is included in Supplemental Figure S2. In addition to this, a single baleen whale detection coincided with the detection of killer whale eDNA, and therefore could be attributed to predation.

The remaining 19 detections (68%) may be attributed to whale poop; while sinking rates and depths of baleen whale poop are generally unknown, we know from expert observations that fecal material from baleen whales is quite large and can sink rapidly from the surface (WHO CAN I PERS COMM FOR THIS?!?). Larger clumps of eDNA can be detectable for up to 2-4 days at the surface (CITE Pedro’s paper here? and/or a Mod 1 paper?), and likely longer at depth where it is colder, darker, and there are fewer microbes digesting cellular material. Research on oceanic particle sinking rate indicates that smaller particles (up to 6 mm) can sink at rates of ~50-300 m day, and that sinking rates increase rapidly with particle size while degradation rates decrease with particle size, with maximum sinking depths in the range of ~800-5,000 m (Giering et al. 2020; McDonnell, Boyd, and Buesseler 2015; Iversen and Ploug 2010).

Based on this, the maximum sinking depth of larger poop particles may far exceed the z range of our study area. It is possible that baleen whale detection rates will continue to increase with increasing depth in some regions due to rapid particle sinking and longer eDNA residency times at depth (REF?). Similarly, detections made near the ocean floor that may be whalefall could also be signals of accumulated whale poop. Genearlly, eDNA is thought to travel less than 10 horizontal km before it is no longer detectable; therefore on a broad scale these detections are likely to reflect the xy distribution of these species but the temporal resolution of detections may be decreased from days to months in colder environments.

Beaked whales were also generally detected deeper than where they are thought to spend most of their time (Figure 2), although still within their normal range of vertical habitat use. Beaked whales spend less of their time near the surface than either baleen whales or delphinids; time-at-depth models estimate that they spend approximately 70% of their time below 100 m, with depth ranges spanning down as far as 3,000 m (CITE Greg paper). While below the surface, beaked whales generally make deep (500+ m) foraging dives interspersed with several shallow (200-300 m) “resting” dives. We found that goose-beaked whales, considered the most common beaked whale in the Pacific Ocean, were most likely to be detected between 200 and 300 m, in the range of their resting dives. Baird’s beaked whales were most likely to be detected at 400-500 m; as 500 m was the extent of our sampling range, it is possible that this species or other beaked whales have higher detection probabilities at depths greater than 500 m. While we did not make predictions of POD by depth for the saber-toothed whale, all three detections of this species were at 300 m (n = 1) and 500 m (n =2).

Finally, for both baleen whales and beaked whales, it is possible that the increase in detections at depth is related to the decreased abundance of competing organisms in the sample. Within each water sample that is collected, cetacean DNA is rare compared to that of microbes, plankton, fishes, etc (CITE from Nastassia’s intro) and may be easily outcompeted for sequencing space in the lab. Deeper in the water column, where microbes, plankton, and fish DNA are likely to be less dense, their masking effect may be lessened enough to decrease the rate of false negatives. We may be able to see this if detections at depth show up in more of the technical replicates than near the surface.

In general, sampling near the surface will increase detection rates of delphinids (and likely porpoises), and sampling at 300-500 m or deeper will increase detection rates of baleen whales and beaked whales. While we found that POD varies by species, we also observed significant trends in POD with depth when species were group by phylogenetic Family or by broad prey category. This indicates some degree of similarity in habitat use across the z-axis within these groups, such that researchers wanting to design an eDNA monitoring study targeting a species for which there is little informaton could use Family or preferred prey to guide their decisions regarding sampling depth.

Question 2:

  1. Occupancy models can be used to separate effects of eDNA presence at the sampling site/in the bottle and detection in the sequence data.

  2. Increasing primer specificity increases POD. POD per station/depth increases ~linearly with technical replication, except that increasing freeze/thaw cycles decreases POD and may counteract the effect of technical replication (for longer target regions, as with Dloop in this study). Shorter regions more robust to freeze/thaw but often have less specificity and lower POD, and/or lower variability and ability to classify to species. Add here how many detections were made at the family level that were dropped.

Patin et al. [Patin2025] used a Bayesian hierarchical occupancy model to characterize delphinid occupancy, capture, and detection across sampling methods and primers in a highly replicated survey in Southern California. The high level of replication (biological and technical) in that study allowed the authors to separately estimate occupancy probability by depth, capture probability by sampling method, and detection probability by primer. Here, we had access to a single biological replicate collected at each sampling site and depth, so we are unable to resolve the difference between occupancy and capture of cetacean eDNA. However, in our study usually five and sometimes as many as eight technical replicates were run on each biological replicate, so we did use this information to better characterize occupancy/capture by depth and detectability by primer.

We found that species-specific patterns of cetacean eDNA presence with depth were qualitatively similar in shape when fitted in an occupancy model context, but the values tended to be much higher, with confidence intervals approaching 1. This makes sense since the Bayesian hierarchical occupancy model was able to separately estimate the probability of presence and the probability of detection, whereas the frequentist GAM was not.

Question 3:

Trends in POD by depth varied geographically by species, as illustrated by the three example species for which we made predictions of depth of maximum POD (Figure 6). At the broad scale of this study, we expect that this at least partially reflects geographic variability in species-specific use of the water column. That may reflect geographic shifts in the depth distribution of prey or preferred prey items of a given predator, which may in turn reflect geographic variability in oceanographic conditions.

Alternatively, it is likely that at least a portion of this observed variability is directly linked to oceanographic variability, and its effect on the fate and transport of eDNA once deposited in the water column. eDNA degradation and residence time will be affected by water temperature, pH, and microbial density. During that time, horizontal transport will be affected by currents, eddies, etc. Vertical transport will be affected by upwelling, mixed layer depth, etc. This study is relatively insensitive to horizontal transport of eDNA, which is expected to travel up to XX km on average before it is no longer detectable (CITE Mod 1 paper here?), our samples were collected at 10 km intervals throughout the study area. Our understanding of the vertical distribution of eDNA is more likely to be affected by these oceanographic processes, which can change sinking rates or residence time.

In both cases, oceanography is an important driver of the 3D distribution of marine mammal eDNA; therefore our next study will focus on the development of 3D spatial distribution models using eDNA and using these models to determine the important oceanographic drivers of marine mammal eDNA in 3D space.

Marine Mammal monitoring: lessons for eDNA experimental design

  1. Sample at depth where species spends most of its time. If this is unknown, can use broad prey category or taxonomic group as a guide.

  2. In laboratory processing, sequence longer fragments first, using as few freeze/thaw cycles as possible. Increasing primer specificity and technical replicates increases probability of detecting a species at a diven location/depth. Shorter fragments that were insensitive to freeze/thaw may be a useful way to increase technical replication but will require more technical replicates than the longer and more specific target regions to acheive the same probability of detection.

  3. We didn’t explicity consider variability in shedding, dispersal, or current in this study, which may affect downstream interpretation of results.

  4. Increasing spatiotemporal sampling resolution could provide insight about 3D habitat use.
